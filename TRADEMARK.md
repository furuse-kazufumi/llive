## What you may do without asking

  * Refer to llive / llmesh / llove by name in articles, blog posts,
    presentations, papers, social media, and similar nominative use.
  * Use the names to truthfully describe that your project, service,
    or tutorial is *about* or *built on top of* llive / llmesh / llove
    ("compatible with", "powered by", "for use with").
  * Distribute unmodified copies of llive under the Apache-2.0 license,
    retaining the original marks in `NOTICE` and source files.

## What requires permission

  * Using "llive", "llmesh", or "llove" as the **name** of your fork,
    derivative, hosted service, or product. Forks should rename
    themselves (e.g. `<yourname>-llive`, `llive-community-edition`).
  * Using the marks in a way that implies official endorsement,
    sponsorship, certification, or partnership.
  * Registering domain names, social-media handles, or trademark
    registrations containing the marks in classes that overlap with the
    Owner's planned use (software, SaaS, AI/LLM tooling).
  * Modifying or stylizing the marks in promotional materials.

To request permission, contact `kazufumi@furuse.work` with a description
of the intended use and the duration.

Once a registration is granted in a given jurisdiction, the appropriate
symbol becomes **®** for that jurisdiction.

## Why a trademark policy?

A liberal open-source license (Apache-2.0) lets anyone use, modify, and
redistribute the code. A trademark policy lets the community trust that
something named "llive" actually behaves like llive — i.e. is the
original project or a permitted release. The two work together: code is
free, the name signals provenance.

This policy is modelled on policies of similar OSS projects (Mozilla,
Python Software Foundation, Apache Software Foundation, Rust Foundation)
and reflects long-standing OSS community norms.
